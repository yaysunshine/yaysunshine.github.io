Quality Questions

27 Jan 2015

Why is the sky blue? What makes a car go? Why do humans not have tails? What is the difference between Ruby and JS?

These are way too broad. A quality question has details and asks exactly what you want to know.

How is light reacting to make the sky the color blue instead of red? Where is the pedal pushing on a car in order for the machine to move? What is the evolution of humans? How are Ruby methods different from JavaScript functions?

These questions are totally random, I know, but my point is to point out (haha) the biggest difference between being specific and being vague.

What it all boils down to (why is this a saying? -- no -- where did this saying come from?): be honest.

What exactly do you want to know?

I apply this to life. Like when a someone is being vague and annoyingly distant, I ask what is up. When someone has a sour look on their face, I ask them what they're thinking. If I want to know how my outfit looks, I ask "how do I look?"

And you know. This is great practice. We'll use the example of "how do I look?"

It's kind of a vague question. I ask it, but then I usually follow up with: "too much eyeliner?", "does my butt look nice and big?", "am I showing too much skin?", "what should I do to my hair to make it less frizzy?", etc. I try to get more specific. When asking someone a question, what do I want them to look at?

Let's now do this to code. I was so annoyed with the bingo challenge. I shouldn't have been, but hey, it happens. I was having a bad couple of weeks.

Anyway, instead of making the entirety of the challenge a problem, I pinpointed what I was having the most trouble with: X-ing out when a called number matched a number on the generated board.

I couldn't tell you how much hair I pulled out of my head over this. Well not exactly literally, but it was hours of "what the fuck do I do?"

So I submitted what I had and then went into office hours. What ended up being the issue was figuring out how to iterate twice.

Simple, I know. But now I know. I struggled, sorted through exactly what was bothering my brain, and verbalized it. And didn't let the question go until I felt secure in my ability to answer my initial question.

Honestly, I so enjoy asking questions. Questions make the world go round. Or at least my learning world. The few times I didn't ask a question that was on my mind, I regretted it. The times when I have verbalized my questions, to anyone for any reason (but mostly in class), I learned! Like. I don't know. I haven't been that silent student. I learn best by asking questions. I'm not scared to do so.

I'm going to share a short story. During my first college English class, I raised my hand, the teacher called on me, and I said, "This might be a stupid question, but --"

And the teacher immediately cut me off saying, "The only stupid question is the one not asked."

Told you it was short. So. I will always remember that moment, and hopefully never not ask a question.

~ Catie Kennedy ~
